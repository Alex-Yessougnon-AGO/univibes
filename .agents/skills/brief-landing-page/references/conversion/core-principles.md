# Core Landing Page Principles

## 1. Headline 4-U Formula

**Rule**: Make headlines Useful, Unique, Urgent, and Ultra-specific

### The Four U's

- **Useful**: Clearly communicates value or benefit
- **Unique**: Differentiates from competitors
- **Urgent**: Creates reason to act now
- **Ultra-specific**: Includes concrete details, numbers, or outcomes

### Why It Matters

- 80% of visitors won't read past the headline
- Each U must be present and compelling
- Ultra-specificity builds immediate relevance

### Testing Checklist

- [ ] Create minimum 10 headline variations
- [ ] Include numbers, outcomes, or specific benefits
- [ ] Match headline to ad copy exactly (message match)
- [ ] A/B test against control until statistical significance

### Headline Formulas

Use these proven patterns:

```
"How to [desired outcome] without [common objection]"
"The [number noun] that [benefit] (even if [objection])"
"Warning: [mistake] could [consequence]"
"Finally, [solution] that [benefit] in [timeframe]"
"The [adjective] way to [outcome] without [pain point]"
```

## 2. Above-Fold Value Proposition

**Rule**: Focus on customer problems, not company story

### Requirements

- Lead with customer pain points, not company history
- Zero scroll required to understand value
- Eliminate company-centric messaging ("We've been in business since...")
- Use customer language from ICP research
- Show transformation, not features
- Include social proof near value prop when possible

### Validation Questions

Ask these about your above-fold content:

1. Can a visitor understand the value in 3 seconds?
2. Does it address their specific problem?
3. Is it written in their language, not corporate speak?

### What to Avoid

❌ "We are a leading provider of..."
❌ "Founded in 2015, our company..."
❌ "Our innovative solution..."

### What to Use

✅ "Stop losing 3 hours a day to..."
✅ "Double your sales calls without..."
✅ "The exact system that generated $2.3M..."

## 3. CTA First-Person Psychology

**Rule**: Use "Get MY guide" vs "Get YOUR guide"

### The Psychology

- First-person language creates personal connection
- 90% more clicks with first-person language
- Reduces psychological distance
- Makes the offer feel more personal and less sales-y

### Best Practices

- Action-oriented verbs (Get, Start, Download, Join)
- Specific and clear (not "Click Here" or "Submit")
- Creates urgency when appropriate
- Matches the value proposition

### Examples

| Weak CTA | Strong CTA |
|----------|------------|
| Submit | Get My Free Guide |
| Click Here | Start My Free Trial |
| Download | Claim My Spot |
| Sign Up | Join My Community |
| Learn More | Show Me How |

## 4. 5-Field Form Maximum

**Rule**: Every additional field kills conversions

### Form Optimization Rules

- Start with essential fields only (name, email minimum)
- Use progressive profiling for additional data
- Build trust before asking for more
- Every field must justify its existence
- Ask: Can this information be collected later?

### Field Priority

1. **Essential**: Email (always required)
2. **High value**: First name (personalization)
3. **Consider carefully**: Phone, company, job title
4. **Usually remove**: Last name, address, "how did you hear about us"

### Optimization Tactics

- Remove unnecessary fields (phone, company unless critical)
- Use smart defaults when possible
- Show progress indicators for multi-step forms
- Provide clear, specific error messages
- Test single-field vs multi-field forms

### Field Count Impact

| Fields | Conversion Impact |
|--------|-------------------|
| 1-2 | Baseline |
| 3 | -10% |
| 4 | -20% |
| 5+ | -25% to -50% |

## 5. Message Match Precision

**Rule**: Ad copy must match landing page headline exactly

### Why Message Match Matters

- Visitors arrive with specific expectations set by the ad
- Broken promises = immediate bounce
- Cognitive dissonance kills conversions
- Trust begins (or ends) in the first 3 seconds

### Message Match Checklist

- [ ] Ad headline matches landing page headline
- [ ] Ad description matches landing page value prop
- [ ] Visual consistency between ad and landing page
- [ ] Offer matches exactly what was promised
- [ ] Tone and language consistent throughout
- [ ] Images/graphics align with ad creative

### Common Failures

❌ Ad says "Free Guide" → Page mentions "Free Ebook"
❌ Ad promises "50% off" → Page shows different discount
❌ Ad shows product A → Page features product B
❌ Ad has blue branding → Page has different colors

### Implementation

1. Create landing pages specific to each ad campaign
2. Use dynamic text replacement where possible
3. Review ad → landing page flow weekly
4. Track bounce rate by traffic source to identify mismatches

## 6. PAS Copy Framework

**Rule**: Problem → Agitate → Solve

### The Framework

1. **Problem**: Lead with the problem (use ICP pain points)
2. **Agitate**: Amplify the pain and consequences
3. **Solve**: Present your solution as the answer

### Structure Template

```
PROBLEM: State the specific problem your audience faces
AGITATE: Amplify the pain — what happens if unsolved? What are they missing?
SOLVE: Your product/service — benefits, proof, call to action
```

### PAS Example

```
PROBLEM:
"You're spending 4 hours a day on email. That's 1,000 hours a year—gone."

AGITATE:
"Meanwhile, your competitors are closing deals and growing. Every hour
in your inbox is an hour they're using to pull ahead."

SOLVE:
"InboxZero uses AI to handle 80% of your emails automatically.
Our customers reclaim 3+ hours daily. Join 10,000+ professionals
who've taken back their time."
```

## 7. Reading Level Grade 6

**Rule**: Smart people prefer simple language

### Guidelines

- **Sentences**: 11 words maximum average
- **Paragraphs**: 2-3 sentences max
- **Words**: Simple, clear, direct
- **Voice**: Active, not passive

### Before/After

- Too complex: "Our comprehensive solution facilitates the optimization of your organizational workflow processes."
- Simple: "Our AI organizes your work. You get more done in less time."

## 8. Benefit-First Language

**Rule**: Features tell, benefits sell

### Translation Formula

```
[Feature] → so you can → [Benefit] → which means → [Emotional Outcome]
```

### Examples

| Feature | Benefit | Emotional Outcome |
|---------|---------|-------------------|
| 24/7 support | Get help anytime | Peace of mind |
| internal | Access anywhere | Freedom and flexibility |
| internal | Works automatically | More free time |

### Writing Patterns

```
"You'll get..." not "We offer..."
"You'll be able to..." not "This allows..."
"Imagine..." not "This product..."
```

## 9. The 5-Question Visitor Diagnostic

**Rule**: Above-fold + first scroll must answer five visitor questions in order

### The Five Questions

Every visitor arrives with five unspoken questions. If the page cannot answer them in sequence, the visitor leaves before the CTA.

1. **What is this?** — One-sentence category clarity. If the visitor cannot describe what you do in their own words after 3 seconds, you fail Q1.
2. **How does it solve my problem?** — The link from product capability to *their* outcome. Not "What does it do?" — "What does it do *for me*?"
3. **Why should I trust you over others?** — Differentiation + proof. The visitor is comparing — make the answer easy.
4. **Who else is using this successfully?** — Specific social proof. Logos, named customers, count of users, named results. Not generic "trusted by thousands."
5. **What do I need to do right now?** — Single, unambiguous next action. If the visitor has to decide between 3 CTAs, they choose zero.

### How This Differs from the 3-Second Test (§2 above-fold + CP-13)

- **3-second test** asks: "Can the visitor *understand* the value in 3 seconds?" — a single comprehension check on the hero
- **5-question diagnostic** asks: "Does the page *answer* the five real visitor questions in order?" — a sequence check across hero + first scroll

The 3-second test catches confusing heros. The 5-question diagnostic catches pages that pass the 3-second test but still bounce because Q2/Q3/Q4 are missing.

### Diagnostic Application

For each candidate page or section spec:

- [ ] Q1 answered? Quote the exact line that does it.
- [ ] Q2 answered? Quote the line linking capability → audience outcome.
- [ ] Q3 answered? Quote the differentiator + proof anchor.
- [ ] Q4 answered? Quote the specific social-proof unit (logo / count / named result).
- [ ] Q5 answered? Identify the single primary CTA + verify no competing CTA in the same viewport.

A "yes" on any question requires a quotable line, not an inferred answer. "It's kind of implied" is a fail.

### Common Failure Modes

| Failure | Symptom | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Q1 unclear | Visitor cannot describe the product after reading the hero | Rewrite hero to lead with category, not metaphor |
| Q2 missing | Page lists features but never says what changes for the visitor | Add a "so you can…" line per feature (see §8) |
| Q3 generic | Differentiation reads as marketing claim, not contrast | Name the alternative being beaten + the specific dimension |
| Q4 thin | Social proof is a generic "trusted by thousands" tagline | Replace with specific named logos, counts, or named outcomes |
| Q5 muddled | Multiple equally-weighted CTAs above the fold | Demote secondary CTAs to lower in the page or to text links |

### Application in lp-brief

This diagnostic is **advisory** at brief-time — surfaced as a section-spec note where appropriate, not a critic FAIL. It runs as a pre-launch readiness check (§12 pre-launch foundation) and as a diagnostic lens during evaluate-landing-page eval-loop scoring. If a section spec scores ≤3/5 on the diagnostic and is not the final pre-launch foundation section, the conversion-critic-agent surfaces an advisory note (not a FAIL) recommending which question to strengthen.
