# Frameworks: Structures

Five proven structural patterns for cold outreach. The strategist picks one per message based on signal strength and context.

## 1. Observation → Problem → Proof → Ask (O→P→P→A)

**When to use:** Signal strength 4-5. You have a specific, observable detail about the prospect (their post, their event, their role change).

**Shape:**
1. **Observation** — one sentence referencing the specific detail
2. **Problem** — one-two sentences on what that detail usually means (the inferred pain)
3. **Proof** — one sentence with named outcome
4. **Ask** — one sentence, tier 1-2 CTA

**Why it works:**
- Passes the remove-the-opener test (the rest of the message depends on the observation)
- Reader sees their world reflected back immediately
- Proof earns the ask rather than just preceding it

**Example:**
```
Your post about SDR attribution being a black box — the "activity not outcomes" line stuck.

That disconnect usually means ~40% of closed-won gets credited to the wrong channel.

Helped Ramp's ops team fix this last quarter — they cut "unknown source" from 38% to 6%.

Worth a look?
```

**Failure modes:**
- Weak observation (signal ≤3) → switch to Q→V→A
- Problem is speculative, not evidenced → reframe or cut
- Proof is generic ("helped many companies") → strengthen or cut

## 2. Question → Value → Ask (Q→V→A)

**When to use:** Signal strength 1-2. No strong observation available. Pain-first framing.

**Shape:**
1. **Question** — one sentence, genuine diagnostic about their likely pain
2. **Value** — 2 sentences on what changes when that pain goes away, plus one proof point
3. **Ask** — one sentence, tier 1-2 CTA

**Why it works:**
- Leads with a reader-centered question (their world, not yours)
- Doesn't fake a signal that isn't there
- The value section provides implicit credentialing without observation-faking

**Example:**
```
Hi Mike,

How much does your team currently spend on lead research before a first call?

Ops teams at your stage usually sink 8-12 hours/week on this before it breaks. We built a tool that pulls the same data in 90 seconds — Linear's team stopped doing pre-call research entirely.

Worth a look?

Thanks,
Mike
```

**Failure modes:**
- Question is generic ("is lead generation a priority?") → make it specific
- Value is about your product, not their outcome → reframe reader-first
- Question followed by "I thought I'd reach out" → delete the transition; the question IS the opener

## 3. Trigger → Insight → Ask

**When to use:** Signal strength 3. A specific event but not a direct quote — funding, hiring, launch, tech-stack change.

**Shape:**
1. **Trigger** — one sentence naming the specific event
2. **Insight** — 1-2 sentences on what that event usually causes (role-tied, stage-tied)
3. **Ask** — one sentence, tier 1-2 CTA

**Why it works:**
- Reader sees you've done specific work (not mass-send)
- Insight demonstrates domain knowledge without a pitch
- Shorter than O→P→P→A — good for LinkedIn DM or length-constrained channels

**Example:**
```
Hi Sarah,

Saw the Series B — congrats.

Usually when ops teams hit this stage, the attribution model stops keeping up with channel expansion. We rebuilt this for 3 Series-B ops teams in the last 6 months — typical time-to-working is 2 weeks.

Want the short overview?

Thanks,
Mike
```

**Failure modes:**
- Trigger is generic (just "I saw your company grew") → strengthen or switch
- Insight is speculative beyond what the trigger supports → pull back
- "Congrats" becomes the whole opener → add substance after it

---

## 4. Story → Bridge → Ask

**When to use:** Community-sell mode, Twitter DM, or when you have a narrative proof that directly maps to their likely situation. Signal variable.

**Shape:**
1. **Story** — 2-3 sentences about a specific client / case / experience
2. **Bridge** — one sentence connecting the story to why you're telling them this
3. **Ask** — one sentence, tier 1-2 CTA

**Why it works:**
- Narrative is more memorable than stat-dump
- Makes you feel human, not vendor-bot
- Works well for less formal channels (Twitter DM, community contexts)

**Example:**
```
Hey Mike,

A founder I worked with last year had the exact problem your "manual lead routing" tweet described — 3 people eyeballing a shared inbox, 40-min average response time.

We built a simple classifier + auto-assignment in make.com. Response time dropped to 4 min. No new hires.

Figured your thread was a signal you'd be interested — want to see the loom?

— Mike
```

**Failure modes:**
- Story is generic ("a client of ours") → name the client or give specifics
- Story is too long (4+ sentences) → cut to the essential arc
- Bridge is forced ("your situation is exactly the same") → soften or trust the pattern-match

## 5. Referral-Mention → Bridge → Ask

**When to use:** When a mutual contact's name is relevant — not a lie, a real connection.

**Shape:**
1. **Referral mention** — one sentence naming the mutual and the context
2. **Bridge** — one sentence connecting to why you're reaching out
3. **Ask** — one sentence, tier 1-2 CTA

**Why it works:**
- Warm-adjacent signal (not cold-cold)
- Opens with credibility borrowed from the mutual
- Lowest-effort framework when the referral exists

**Example:**
```
Hi Sarah,

Patrick mentioned your work on the onboarding redesign when we talked last week — he said you were thinking about the mid-funnel drop-off problem.

Worked on the same thing with 2 Series-B SaaS teams — wrote up the pattern [here]. Happy to share more if useful.

Worth a look?

Thanks,
Mike
```

**Failure modes:**
- "Patrick" is invented or a stretch (they met once at an event) → don't use this framework
- Bridge is pitch-shaped ("so I wanted to introduce our product") → rewrite as continuation of the mutual's context
- Name-dropping without permission (if the mutual objects, burns the relationship) → verify first

## Framework Selection Decision Tree

These routings are signal-based starting points, not hard rules — trust a better read of the prospect and note the departure.

```
Start: What's the signal strength?
│
├─ 4-5 (specific quote/post/event): Observation → Problem → Proof → Ask
│
├─ 3 (specific but general event — funding, hiring): Trigger → Insight → Ask
│
├─ 1-2 (no specific signal) AND no mutual contact: Question → Value → Ask
│
├─ Any, AND a real mutual contact exists: Referral-Mention → Bridge → Ask
│
└─ Community-sell mode OR Twitter DM OR strong narrative proof: Story → Bridge → Ask
```

## Rules That Apply to All Frameworks

- **One CTA, one ask, tier 1-2 for touch 1.**
- **Reader's world dominates over sender's.** First sentence after salutation is about them, their event, their question — not about you.
- **Proof must be specific or absent.** Generic proof ("many leading companies") is worse than no proof. If you don't have it, drop the Proof step.
- **Peer voice, contractions, cut filler.** Applies regardless of framework.
- **Don't mix frameworks.** If you find yourself doing "observation → question → proof" you've combined two frameworks into one — pick one, commit, cut.

## Anti-Patterns (framework-wide)

- **Template-fatigue patterns.** If 10 emails in a row all use O→P→P→A, readers pattern-match to template even when individual emails are well-written. Vary framework across a multi-touch sequence.
- **Framework cargo-culting.** Following the structure without checking if it serves this specific message. If none of the frameworks fit, write freeform — frameworks are tools, not rules.
- **Framework-as-scaffolding.** Producing 4 paragraphs because the framework has 4 steps even when 2 would work. Cut steps that aren't earning their place.
