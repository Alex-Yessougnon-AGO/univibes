# Channel: Twitter/X

Two sub-formats: **public reply** (visible to everyone) and **DM** (private). Different craft.

## The Twitter/X Ecosystem

Unlike email or LinkedIn, Twitter/X is a **demand-signal** channel — people post publicly about problems, complaints, and needs in real time. The right Twitter outreach is often a public reply that adds value, followed (maybe) by a DM.

**Best demand signals to watch for:**

| Signal | Examples | What to do |
|--------|----------|------------|
| Problem statement | "Why is [X] so hard to track?" / "Anyone else tired of [Y]?" | Reply with value: answer, link, or observation |
| internal | "What's a good alternative to [competitor]?" | Reply with your tool + one-line differentiator (only if you actually compete) |
| "Looking for recs" | "Need a tool that does [X]" | Reply with recommendation + brief why |
| Complaint thread | "This [competitor] change is brutal" | Reply with empathy + optional mention |
| Build-in-public question | "How did you solve [X] at [scale]?" | Reply with substance; not a pitch |

**Anti-signals (do NOT reply):**
- Personal venting unrelated to your product
- Political content, hot takes
- Anyone's grief or loss
- Posts from accounts with no context (might be parody/bot)

## Public Reply Craft

### Constraints

- 1-2 sentences (Twitter is fast; long replies get skipped)
- Character limit is 280, but aim for under 180 for mobile readability
- No CTAs in the public reply itself
- Your @handle shows — they can see your bio/product immediately
- First comment gets the most visibility; quoting or late-thread replies rarely seen

### Rules

- **Answer the question or add value first.** Even if you have a product that solves this, lead with substance.
- **Mention your tool once, optionally, and clearly flag it.** "I built something for this — [tool name]. Happy to show." The transparency matters.
- **Do not pitch in public reply.** Save pitches for DM (if they reach out) or for a separate touch.
- **Do not argue.** Twitter amplifies conflict; you're playing the long game.

### Patterns That Work

**Example — problem statement:**
```
Prospect tweet: "Why is attribution so hard? Every dashboard I look at says something different."

Good reply:
Two things usually cause it — (1) UTM conflict across channels and (2) server-side events firing before client-side. Fixing one without the other just shifts the noise around. I built a tool for this ([@tool]) — happy to walk through the setup if useful.
```

**Example — alt-seeking:**
```
Prospect tweet: "Anyone know a good alternative to Clearbit now that it's been acquired?"

Good reply:
Been testing [tool] for similar use cases — the data freshness is better than Clearbit for tech stack signals, and the pricing is usable at under-500-headcount scale. Happy to share what worked/didn't.
```

### Patterns That Kill

- "GREAT QUESTION! We can help!" (self-promo, no value)
- "DM me and I'll send you info" (no substance in public)
- Multiple tweets in a thread responding to one tweet (spam pattern)
- Quote-tweeting their complaint with your pitch (hostile)
- "As someone who works at [company]..." (adversarial vibe)
- "👀" / "🚀" / "This is why [company] exists" (empty)

## DM Craft

Only DM AFTER a public exchange (reply + their response) or if they have "DMs open — pitch me" in bio.

### Constraints

- 2-4 sentences
- Casual register (like texting a colleague you've met once)
- Link okay if relevant (Twitter doesn't deprioritize DMs with links the way LinkedIn does)
- One CTA, tier 1-2

### Teaser Preview Constraint (DM)

X DM inboxes preview only the **first ~40-55 characters** before requiring a click. Public replies don't have this constraint — they render inline. DM is where the rule bites.

> "Teaser preview ~40-55 chars." — internal synthesis

**Practical implications:**
- The first 40-55 chars must carry the trigger reference or the curiosity hook, not a salutation. The "no salutation" convention in §Rules below already pulls in this direction — the teaser window reinforces it.
- Bury anything heavy past ~55 chars; the visible slice should make them want to click in.

Pattern basis: internal research synthesis.

### Rules

- **Reference the tweet/exchange.** Twitter DM in a vacuum feels random.
- **Keep the same voice they used.** If they're casual, be casual. If they're terse, be terse.
- **Optional: no salutation.** Twitter DMs often open without "Hi X" — feels more like a text.

### Patterns That Work

```
followed up on your tweet about UTM conflict — made this loom showing the fix in 3 min: [link]. curious if any of it maps to your setup, or if you're seeing something different.
```

```
saw your thread on dashboard drift. been working on the same problem — put together a teardown of how Ramp's team rebuilt theirs. want me to send it over?
```

### Anti-Patterns

- Formal email-style DMs ("Dear Sarah, I hope this message finds you well...") — breaks register hard
- Pitching without prior exchange if their bio doesn't invite it
- Sending a long pitch that reads like an email body-pasted
- Asking for a meeting in the first DM without prior exchange
- Using Twitter DM to bypass someone who already said "not interested" on another channel

## Platform-Native Voice

Twitter has a distinct register. Cold outreach that reads like an email sent to Twitter fails.

| Twitter register | Email register |
|-----------------|----------------|
| "saw your thread on X" | "I came across your post about X" |
| "took me a bit to figure out" | "In our experience, we've found..." |
| "want me to send the teardown?" | "Would you be interested in receiving..." |
| "no pressure" | "Please let me know at your convenience" |

Peer, casual, short. Not rude — just not business-email.

## Anti-Patterns (Twitter-wide)

- **Public reply then immediate DM before they responded.** Pushy. Wait for them to engage publicly first.
- **Using a Twitter account with no posts.** Your bio/history IS your credibility; blank accounts get ignored or blocked.
- **Responding to old tweets (90+ days).** Context is dead; reads as data-scraped.
- **Replying to every tweet in a thread.** You only need to respond to the main tweet — scattered replies look like spam.
- **Using Twitter for services-sell pitches to enterprise targets.** Twitter works best for SMB, indie hackers, and creator/founder personas. For enterprise buyers, LinkedIn or email wins.
