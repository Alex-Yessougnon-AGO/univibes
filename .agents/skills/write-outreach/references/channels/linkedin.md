# Channel: LinkedIn

Three sub-formats with different craft rules: **connection note** / **post-accept DM** / **InMail (premium)**.

## Connection Note

**Constraint: ≤300 characters.** Hard limit.

### Teaser Preview Constraint

Beyond the 300-char hard limit, LinkedIn's inbox preview surfaces only the **first ~50-55 characters** before the recipient clicks in. The rest is hidden until open. Optimize the opening clause for this window — anything past ~55 chars is post-click, not open-bait.

> "Teaser visible at ~50-55 chars. Shorter first name = more teaser room." — internal synthesis

**Practical implications:**
- A `Hi [pattern-derived] — ` opener can burn 20+ of the 55-char preview on greeting. Lead with the trigger noun when the first name runs long.
- The ≤300-char hard limit on the full note still applies; this is a layered constraint on the *visible* slice.

Pattern basis: internal research synthesis.

### Rules

- **No pitch.** Ever. This is a handshake, not a sales call.
- **Reference something specific.** Their post, a mutual connection, a shared interest, their role at a company you follow.
- **One clause on why you're connecting.** Not "to grow my network." Something specific.
- **No URL.** Takes space, reduces accept rate.
- **Sign-off optional.** Often looks more natural without one.

### Patterns That Work

```
Hi Sarah — your post on attribution pipelines landed. Been thinking about the activity-vs-outcomes gap a lot. Would love to connect.
```
(180 chars — references post, shares thinking, no pitch)

```
Hi Mike — saw you're building the ops function at Linear from scratch. Went through the same thing at my last company. Would love to connect and compare notes someday.
```
(182 chars — specific role+context, peer-framing, soft optionality)

```
Hi Jess — Patrick mentioned your work on the onboarding redesign. Would love to connect.
```
(96 chars — name-drop a mutual, one clause, done)

### Patterns That Kill Accept Rate

- "I'd love to learn more about what you do at [company]." — generic; anyone could send this
- "I'm building X and wanted to introduce myself." — sender-first, no value
- "Quick question — do you handle [X]?" — a sales question disguised as a connection
- "Let's connect!" alone — zero signal
- Templates with {{FirstName}} pattern visible in phrasing
- Pitching in the note ("We help companies like yours...")
- Links in the note

### Connection Note vs. Nothing

Often better to send the connection request with NO note than a weak note. An empty request accepts at 20-30%. A bad note accepts at 10-15%. Only send a note if you have a specific, non-pitchy reference to include.

## Post-Accept DM

Sent shortly after they accept the connection request.

### Constraints

- 3-5 sentences total
- One CTA, tier 1-2
- No URLs if possible (LinkedIn deprioritizes DMs with links)
- No images, no emojis (1 max if native to your voice)

### Timing

- **Same day OR within 24 hours** after accept: optimal — they still remember why they accepted
- **2-3 days later**: still fine, but reference the connection
- **A week later**: fine if you have a new trigger; otherwise stale

### Patterns That Work

**If connection note mentioned a post:**
```
Thanks for connecting, Sarah.

Been thinking about that "activity vs outcomes" line from your attribution post. Worked with Ramp's ops team on exactly this — they cut unknown-source credit from 38% to 6% without a replatform.

Worth sharing the short teardown with you? Happy to send.

Thanks,
Mike
```

**If connection was a mutual:**
```
Thanks for the accept, Jess.

Patrick spoke highly of the onboarding redesign. If you're ever dealing with the mid-onboarding drop-off problem, I put together a teardown of how three Series-B teams solved it — happy to share.

No pressure — just saw the overlap.

Thanks,
Mike
```

**If cold accept, no prior signal:**
```
Thanks for connecting, [Name].

Saw you're leading ops at [company]. If you're heads-down on [common pain for their role/stage], I've been in the weeds on the same thing — happy to swap notes some time.

No agenda.

Thanks,
[sender]
```

### Anti-Patterns

- **Diving straight into pitch in the first post-accept DM.** They accepted because the connection note was light-touch. Heavy DM breaks that compact.
- **"Do you have 15 mins this week?"** as the first post-accept ask. Too heavy too early.
- **Long DMs (7+ sentences).** LinkedIn DMs are shorter than email; users scroll less.
- **Multiple URLs.** DMs with 2+ links get deprioritized in the inbox.
- **Using the DM to send a "one-pager" attachment.** Feels like spam.

## InMail (Premium/Sales Navigator)

InMails cost credits and get higher visibility, but accept/reply norms are different.

### Constraints

- Subject line (200 chars max — use 30-60)
- Body 2-4 short paragraphs
- One CTA

### Rules

- **Subject line is more visible than email subject.** Worth investing.
- **Treat like a shorter email, not a DM.** Longer than DM, tighter than email.
- **InMail reply rates are ~10-25% average** — higher than cold email for the right persona, but you're paying per send. Make it count.
- **Don't apologize for using InMail.** "Sorry for the cold reach" is common and weak.

### Pattern

```
Subject: ramp's attribution rebuild

Hi Sarah,

Your post about SDR attribution being a black box — "we track activity not outcomes" stuck. That usually means ~40% of closed-won is credited wrong.

Helped Ramp's ops team rebuild their model — they cut "unknown source" from 38% to 6%.

Worth a 15-min compare?

Thanks,
Mike
```

## Cross-Format Rules

- **Don't pitch on three channels at once.** LinkedIn DM + email + InMail in the same week is spam behavior. Pick a primary and let the others serve as reinforcement only.
- **Channel-specific etiquette beats email etiquette when they conflict.** Email "Best regards" is fine; LinkedIn "Best regards" reads stiff.
- **If they accept the connection but don't reply to the DM**, let 7-10 days pass before follow-up. LinkedIn is a slower channel than email.

## Anti-Patterns (LinkedIn-wide)

- Mass-connecting without notes, then mass-DM'ing pitches after accept (detected by LinkedIn, accounts flagged)
- Pasting an email body into a DM — register is off
- Using LinkedIn voice notes for cold outreach (read as invasive)
- Links to Calendly in the first post-accept DM (feels transactional)
- Emojis in every line (⚡ 🚀 📊 — looks automated)
