## 9-Channel Framework

The complete channel map for growth. Evaluate every channel for relevance — don't default to digital-only.

### Channel Map

| # | Channel | Organic Tactics | Paid Tactics | Typical Role |
|---|---------|----------------|--------------|--------------|
| 1 | **Search engines/AEO** | SEO, AEO (AI search optimization) | Search ads (Google, Bing), Shopping ads | Acquisition (intent capture) |
| 2 | **Store/Listing platforms** | ASO (App Store/Play Store), G2/Capterra profiles, Product Hunt | Apple Search ads, Google Play ads, sponsored listings | Acquisition (marketplace discovery) |
| 3 | **Bounty/Info platforms** | Referral programs, affiliate networks | Incentive campaigns, bounty programs, paid referrals | Acquisition (incentivized) |
| 4 | **News** | Earned press coverage, contributed articles | Paid PR articles, guest-postings, press releases | Awareness + Trust |
| 5 | **Forums/Communities** | Threading (Reddit, VOZ, HN, Quora), community engagement | Paid ads (Reddit ads, forum sponsorships) | Awareness + Trust + Advocacy |
| 6 | **Social media** | Organic brand content, affiliate content, UGC (influencers/creators), Internal UGC (mass accounts, clippings) | Paid ads (Meta, X, LinkedIn, TikTok), boosted posts | Full funnel |
| 7 | **IRL** | Events, conferences, meetups, word of mouth | OOH (billboards, transit), Point of Sales materials, event sponsorships | Awareness + Relationship |
| 8 | **Mailbox** | Transactional emails, newsletters, onboarding sequences | Paid inbox ads (Gmail Sponsored Promotions) | Nurture + Conversion |
| 9 | **SMS** | Transactional SMS, order updates | Paid SMS campaigns, sponsorship SMS | Conversion + Re-engagement |

### Channel Selection by Growth Motion

| Channel | PLG Priority | SLG Priority | Why |
|---------|-------------|-------------|-----|
| Search engines/AEO | **High** — captures problem-aware searchers | **High** — captures intent | Both motions need search visibility |
| Store/Listing platforms | **High** — if product is app-based | **Medium** — for marketplace presence | PLG relies on self-serve discovery |
| Bounty/Info platforms | **Medium** — referral is PLG lever | **Medium** — affiliate extends reach | PLG: organic referral > paid incentive |
| News | **Low** — unless raising funding/launching | **Medium** — builds trust for enterprise | SLG needs credibility signals |
| Forums/Communities | **High** — builds community, advocacy | **Low** — unless ICP lives there | PLG: community IS the growth engine |
| Social media | **Medium** — organic + UGC | **High** — paid + outbound | SLG: paid social drives leads |
| IRL | **Low** — unless at events | **High** — conferences, demos, POS | SLG: relationship-driven sales |
| Mailbox | **High** — onboarding + activation | **High** — nurture + outbound | Both motions need email |
| SMS | **Low** — unless transactional | **Medium** — follow-ups, campaigns | SLG: direct response channel |

### Channel Evaluation Checklist

For each candidate channel, assess:

1. **ICP Habitat Match** — Does the ICP live here? (from research-icp habitat maps)
2. **Cost Model** — Paid, organic, or hybrid? What's the expected CAC contribution?
3. **Timeline to Impact** — How long until this channel delivers results? (Search: months. Paid ads: days. Community: quarters.)
4. **Resource Requirements** — Team size, content volume, tools, budget needed
5. **Measurability** — Can we track attribution? (Digital: high. OOH/IRL: low — needs proxy metrics)
6. **Scalability** — Can we increase spend/effort and get proportional returns?

## Content Classification: Searchable vs. Shareable

Every piece of content serves one of two demand modes. This is orthogonal to the pillar system — any pillar can produce both types.

| Type | Purpose | Characteristics | Distribution |
|------|---------|----------------|--------------|
| **Searchable** | Capture existing demand | Keyword-driven, answers known questions, evergreen, SEO-optimized | Blog, YouTube, Quora, SEO landing pages |
| **Shareable** | Create new demand | Novel insights, contrarian takes, original data, emotional hooks | Social, email, KOLs, communities |

**Why this matters:** Searchable content compounds over time (traffic grows). Shareable content spikes and decays (engagement-driven). A healthy content mix needs both — pure searchable builds slow, pure shareable requires constant production.

**Assignment rule:** For each angle in the Angle Bank, tag it as Searchable, Shareable, or Both. Then match to channels:
- Searchable angles → owned blog, YouTube, SEO-focused channels
- Shareable angles → social, community, KOL, email
- Both → prioritize for highest-effort production (these are your best content investments)

## Habitat-Informed Channel Strategy (Enhanced Mode)

When you have ICP personas with Audience Habitat Maps, use the habitat data to drive channel selection rather than guessing.

### Step 1: Import Habitat Data

Pull the Audience Habitat Map from ICP research for each persona:

| Persona | Channel Native | Channel Type | Channel Name | Density | Engagement Type | Habitat Role |
|---------|----------------|--------------|--------------|---------|-----------------|--------------|
| [Persona A] | [Platform] | [Community kind] | [Specific community] | [H/M/L] | [Lurker/Engager/Creator/Subscriber/Searcher] | [Discovery/Trust/Conversion] |

If habitat data is not available, fall back to Quick ICP Question 15 to capture it.

### Step 2: Habitat-to-Channel Strategy Translation

For each habitat entry, translate it into an IMC channel decision:

| Persona | Habitat (from ICP) | IMC Channel Decision | Rationale |
|---------|-------------------|---------------------|-----------|
| [Persona A] | LinkedIn → Industry groups → "SaaS Growth Leaders" (H density, Engager) | Owned: thought leadership posts | High-density habitat, audience engages actively — ideal for reach angles |
| [Persona A] | Reddit → Subreddits → r/startups (M density, Lurker) | Content: SEO-optimized articles | Persona lurks here — use for trust-building content, not engagement hooks |
| [Persona B] | Email → Newsletters → "Lenny's Newsletter" (H density, Subscriber) | Partner: sponsored placement | Persona subscribes to curated content — align angle with newsletter format |

**Translation rules:**
- **H density + Engager/Creator** → High-priority channel, allocate reach and engagement angles
- **H density + Lurker** → Use for awareness/trust content, don't expect engagement metrics
- **M density + Searcher** → SEO/content opportunity, optimize for discoverability
- **L density** → Deprioritize unless it's a high-trust conversion habitat

### Step 3: Habitat-Informed Channel Strategy per Persona

Map habitats to the discovery → trust → conversion funnel:

| Persona | Discovery (found via) | Trust-Building (validates at) | Conversion (acts at) |
|---------|----------------------|------------------------------|---------------------|
| [Persona A] | [Habitat: Channel Name] | [Habitat: Channel Name] | [Habitat: Channel Name] |
| [Persona B] | [Habitat: Channel Name] | [Habitat: Channel Name] | [Habitat: Channel Name] |

For each row, uses the specific habitat entry that justifies the channel choice.

**Platform behavior definitions:**
- **Lurker**: Consumes but rarely engages publicly
- **Engager**: Likes, comments, shares regularly
- **Creator**: Posts own content, active in discussions
- **Subscriber**: Prefers push content (email, notifications)
- **Searcher**: Uses search when problems arise (SEO opportunity)

## Growth Loops (Acquisition Mechanics That Compound)

Most channel work is **flow** — money in, traffic out, dependent on continued spend. A growth loop is the alternative: a mechanic where the output of one cycle becomes the input to the next, so each acquired user has the potential to produce more acquired users without proportional marketing spend.

This subsection covers three loops that compose cleanly with the 9-channel framework. They are mechanics, not channels — each one runs through one or more of the 9 channels.

### Loop Type 1 — Tiered Milestone Referral

**Shape:** an existing user invites peers; rewards unlock at named invite counts, not per-invite.

**Why tiered milestones, not per-invite incentives:**
- Per-invite ("get $5 per friend") incentivizes a long tail of low-quality invites — recipients with no real fit
- Milestone tiers ("invite 5 friends = free product A; invite 10 = free product B; invite 25 = full subscription year") create a coordination pressure: the inviter is committed by the time they've sent 4 invites and wants the unlock, so the 5th, 6th, 7th invite has earned momentum
- The reward at each tier should be product-shaped, not cash — product rewards extend the operator's footprint with the invitee, cash rewards extend the inviter's wallet but say nothing about the product

**Composition rules:**

- **3 tiers minimum, 5 max.** Two tiers (e.g., 5 / 25) leaves a long dead zone between unlocks; six tiers becomes noise. Pick 3 (low / medium / high) or 4 (low / medium / high / aspirational).
- **First tier is reachable within 24-48h of receiving the referral link** — usually 3-5 invites. If tier 1 takes a week to reach, most users abandon before getting any unlock-momentum.
- **Each tier's reward must be visibly larger than the prior tier**, not a sidegrade. "Tier 1: 10% off; tier 2: 15% off" reads as fake escalation. Stronger: "Tier 1: 10% off; tier 2: free starter product; tier 3: full subscription."
- **Leaderboard (optional but powerful)** — surfacing the top inviters in a public-facing leaderboard fires social-status psychology. Honest only if the leaderboard is real-time and the top spots can actually be reached by a determined participant. Fake leaderboards are detected within a week.
- **Reward delivery must be automated and within minutes of unlock.** A reward that requires the inviter to email support to claim kills the loop dead.

**When this loop fits:**
- Product has a natural peer-use case (productivity tool, consumer subscription, online service used in groups)
- Operator can afford the unit economics — reward cost per invitee must be less than the LTV gain from the invitee's eventual conversion
- The invite mechanism is low-friction (link, not "have your friend visit and use your code")
- Pre-launch / waitlist is the strongest moment for this loop — exclusivity itself is the lever

**When this loop fails:**
- Product has no peer-use case (most B2B enterprise sales — invitees can't act on the referral without a budget approval cycle)
- Reward delivery is gated by human review (creates a backlog the operator can't service)
- Audience already has a transactional relationship with the brand (existing customers expect rewards; referral becomes the floor, not a lift)

**Anti-patterns:**
- **Untiered "get $X per friend" cash bounty** — drives invite quantity, destroys invite quality. Recipient experience is "got a spammy DM from someone I barely know."
- **Tier rewards that exceed product margin** — the operator gives away a year of subscription for 25 invites, the invites convert at 5%, the operator loses money per invite. Run the unit economics before launch.
- **Manual reward delivery** — at any meaningful scale (>50 unlocks/day), manual delivery becomes the bottleneck and the loop dies.
- **Performance promises in plan copy** — never tell the operator the loop will "acquire N emails in M days." The achievable rate is a function of the existing audience size, the product fit, and the reward generosity, none of which the plan-campaign can know in advance.

### Loop Type 2 — Content-Earned Distribution

**Shape:** the content asset itself is good enough to be shared by its target audience, so distribution compounds without paid amplification.

This is not "make content go viral." It's a specific architectural choice — produce content where the audience's *recommendation* of the content is part of the operator's brand argument. Two patterns:

| Pattern | Shape | Example pattern |
|---|---|---|
| **Category-leading reference** | One asset becomes the canonical answer to a high-intent query | "The {category} pricing guide" or "The {tool} comparison matrix that ranks every option" |
| **Original data drop** | Operator publishes data nobody else has, citation-grade | An internal benchmark, a survey result, a custom-built calculator |

**Composition rules:**

- **The asset must be evergreen-shaped** — pricing pages, comparison matrices, calculators, definitive guides. Time-stamped news posts don't earn re-distribution.
- **The operator's perspective must be present but not promotional** — the asset is the value; the operator's brand is the credit, not the headline.
- **The link must be linkable** — clean URL, social-card metadata, single anchor for the asset (not a paywall, not a login wall).
- **Updates must be visible** — a "last updated {date}" line near the top, paired with a versioned changelog at the bottom, signals that the asset is maintained.

**Composes with:** Searchable content type from the table above; News channel (the asset can be cited by journalists if the data is original); Forums/Communities (people share canonical references in answer threads).

### Loop Type 3 — Product-Embedded Distribution

**Shape:** the product's own use surface produces shareable artifacts, so usage drives acquisition.

**Examples of the shape (not vendor endorsements — the pattern):**
- A design tool that watermarks its export with a "Made with {tool}" footer
- A presentation tool whose share link surfaces the tool's branding
- A doc tool where embedded documents on third-party sites carry the tool's name
- A workflow tool whose public outputs have the tool's affordance visible

**Composition rules:**

- **The watermark/footer must be removable on paid plans.** This is the loop's monetization seam — free users do the distribution work; paid users buy out of the distribution. If the watermark cannot be removed, the loop reads as forced advertising and erodes paid conversion.
- **The exposed surface must be the user's content, not the operator's marketing.** "Made with {tool}" works; "Sign up for {tool} now!" inserted into the user's output destroys the user's perception.
- **The product surface must be high-quality on its own merits.** Watermarked low-quality output associates the tool with bad work. The loop multiplies whatever signal the user's output already carries.

**Composes with:** Mostly Social media + IRL channels (wherever the user shares their output). Not really a content-channel mechanic.

### Loop Selection Guidance

Most operators pick one loop and run it. Stacking two loops is fine if they don't compete for the same user attention; stacking three is usually one loop too many. The plan-campaign should commit to a primary loop and a secondary loop at most.

| Loop | Fits early-stage | Fits mature-stage | PLG fit | SLG fit |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Tiered milestone referral | ✓ (waitlist phase) | ✓ (existing-customer expansion) | ✓ | Mostly no |
| Content-earned distribution | ✓ (when the operator has expertise) | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ |
| Product-embedded distribution | ✓ (if product naturally has shareable surface) | ✓ | ✓ | Rarely |

## Pillar-to-Derivative Distribution (Content Lifecycle Repurposing)

The Searchable vs Shareable table above is the *type* lens. This subsection is the *lifecycle* lens — how one pillar piece of content becomes many distributed derivatives without the operator producing new content from scratch every cycle.

### The Composition Pattern

| Stage | Asset Type | Effort | Channel Native |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Pillar (origin asset)** | Long-form research, deep guide, original data, or interview (1,500-3,500 words, OR a 20-40min video, OR a multi-hour podcast) | High — the only meaningful effort cycle | Owned blog, YouTube, podcast feed |
| **Atomic derivatives** | 3-5 social threads (X / LinkedIn / Threads), 2-4 short-form video scripts (TikTok / Reels / Shorts), 1-2 carousel posts, 1 newsletter | Medium — extraction, not authoring | Social, email |
| **Visual derivatives** | 2-4 infographics, 1-2 quote-cards, 1 chart screenshot package | Medium — design, not authoring | Social (Instagram, Pinterest), social proof in body content |
| **Community seeding** | 3-6 contextual replies in relevant communities (Reddit, Hacker News, Slack/Discord groups, Quora), each linking the pillar | Low — selective insertion | Forums/Communities (channel 5) |

The rule: **one pillar produces ~10-15 derivatives** over a 2-4 week distribution window. Past that window, derivatives stop earning attention and the next pillar should be in production.

### Per-Derivative Rules

- **Each derivative stands alone.** A reader who only sees the derivative — never visits the pillar — should get value. If the derivative is purely a "click for the rest" teaser, engagement craters and the operator burns trust.
- **Each derivative links back to the pillar.** This is the second seam — derivatives feed pillar traffic (which earns search authority), pillar feeds derivative credibility ("this thread is an excerpt from the full guide").
- **Derivative voice matches channel native.** X-thread voice ≠ LinkedIn post voice ≠ short-form video script ≠ newsletter section. The pillar's voice is the *operator's voice*; each derivative's voice is the *channel's voice* through the operator.
- **Visual derivatives use real data points from the pillar.** Infographic content invented for the visual that wasn't in the pillar reads as decorative; visuals tied to the pillar's specific claims compound the pillar's authority.

### Sequencing the Distribution Window

| Day | Move |
|---|---|
| Day 0 | Publish pillar |
| Day 1-3 | First 2-3 atomic derivatives (highest-priority social platforms first) |
| Day 4-7 | Visual derivatives + newsletter syndication |
| Day 7-14 | Remaining atomic derivatives + community seeding |
| Day 14-28 | Re-surface the strongest 1-2 derivatives (re-share the thread that worked, re-cut the video that performed) |
| Day 28+ | Stop pushing; let the pillar earn passive search traffic |

### Anti-Patterns

- **Pillar without derivatives.** The operator publishes a great long-form piece, links it once in their newsletter, and moves on. ~80% of the available distribution leverage goes unused.
- **All derivatives published in 48 hours.** Saturates the audience, all derivatives compete for the same week of attention; later derivatives get no oxygen.
- **Derivatives that contradict the pillar.** Common when the social-team and the long-form team don't coordinate — the X-thread says "5 reasons", the LinkedIn post says "7 reasons", the pillar says "4 reasons." Pick one number and hold across all derivatives.
- **Pillar selection driven by what's easy to derive, not what's worth saying.** The operator picks safe topics because derivative volume looks high; the pillar's argument is generic and so are the derivatives.
- **Quoting case-study numbers from outside the pillar.** Derivatives sometimes drift into citing performance numbers ("brands using this approach see X% lift") that the pillar never claimed and the operator never verified. Stay inside the pillar's evidence boundary.
- **Re-running the same pillar's distribution window 3+ times.** After ~6 weeks, even the best pillar is exhausted in its primary audience. Repeated re-distribution trains the audience to ignore the operator's social channels.
