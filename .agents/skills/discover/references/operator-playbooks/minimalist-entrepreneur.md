# Operator Playbook — The Minimalist Entrepreneur

This playbook loads on **every** non-trivial discover invocation regardless of domain. It is **stance, not domain knowledge** — the minimalist-entrepreneur minimalist-entrepreneur frame ("processize before productize · sell before scale · profitability is the goal · spend time before money") is a posture for evaluating ANY proposed direction, not just SaaS or DTC. When a user opens discover with an idea, a plan, a feature, a launch, or a scope question, this playbook gives the discover skill a bias check: are we about to over-build, over-spend, over-launch, or over-scope?

The frame is conservative on input ("can you do this manually?") and aggressive on validation ("has anyone paid you yet?"). The whole point is to push back **before** capital and time are committed — which is exactly when discover runs.

---

## 1. Core Frame

**Validation happens through selling, not building.** [pattern-derived] Most founders spend months building a product nobody wants. The minimalist alternative: validate by selling a manual version of your solution first, then automate only what you've proven works manually.

Two compounding theses sit underneath:

1. **Processize before you productize.** [pattern-derived] Every great product started as someone doing the work by hand. the creator-commerce company started with the operator collecting PayPal emails and sending payments to creators one by one. The "product" was the operator doing it manually. *"Most apps on the internet are just forms and lists."* [pattern-derived]

2. **Profitability is the goal — not growth, not scale, not VC dollars.** [pattern-derived] *"Profitability gets you off the grid. Then you grow mindfully with unlimited runway."* [pattern-derived] Reversibility is a first-class concern; long leases, big hires, and VC term sheets are irreversible decisions and should be treated as such.

The frame's load-bearing rejection: it says **NO to launch theater, waitlists as validation, growth-at-all-costs, and "build it and they will come."** It treats *interest* as worthless and *transactions* as the only real signal. Free users give zero signal — *"There is a massive difference between free and $1."* [pattern-derived]

When discover loads this frame, it is asking: is the user about to make an irreversible bet on a hypothesis they could have tested for $0 by doing the work manually for ten people first?

## 2. The Eight Principles

These are the eight evaluation lenses from `minimalist-review` [pattern-derived]. Each is a question discover can throw at any plan. Verbatim where indicated.

### Principle 1 — Community First

> *"Does this serve your community? Or is it driven by ego, vanity metrics, or what 'successful companies' do? Are you staying close to your customers? Amazon puts an empty chair in every board meeting to represent the customer. Are you doing the equivalent?"* [pattern-derived]

**Cognitive move:** Before evaluating *what* to build, evaluate *who* you're serving and whether you have access to them. A community-less plan is a plan optimizing for a hypothetical user.

**When this fires in discover:** User describes a target market in category language ("SMBs," "developers," "creators") without naming a community they belong to or have access to. Push back: *who specifically — and where do they hang out?*

### Principle 2 — Start Manual, Then Automate

> *"Are you over-building? Could this be done manually first? 'Processize' before you 'productize.' Have you done this by hand enough times to know it works?"* [pattern-derived]

**Cognitive move:** Treat every product as a manual service first. Strip the technology away — if you had nothing but a phone, an inbox, and a spreadsheet, how would you deliver this value?

**When this fires in discover:** User is talking about architecture, framework choice, database, UI before they've delivered the value once by hand. Push back: *have you done this manually for ten people? If not, what are we automating?*

### Principle 3 — Build As Little As Possible

> *"Can you ship this in a weekend? What's the simplest version that makes someone's life better? Are you building for today's customers or hypothetical future ones?"* [pattern-derived]

**Cognitive move:** Default-reduce scope. The first iteration should be prototyped in 2-3 days [pattern-derived]. If you can't ship in a weekend, reduce scope until you can. *"Can I ship it in a weekend?" If not, reduce scope until you can.* [pattern-derived]

**When this fires in discover:** Plan involves building infrastructure, abstractions, or features for users who don't exist yet.

### Principle 4 — Sell Before You Scale

> *"Have real people paid real money for this? Are you trying to market before you've sold? (Sales comes first, marketing second.) Manual sales = 99% of early growth."* [pattern-derived]

**Cognitive move:** Sales is a verb done by the founder, in DMs and emails, before any marketing channel is touched. Marketing is automation of selling — you can't automate something you haven't done manually.

**When this fires in discover:** User describes a launch plan, growth plan, or marketing plan before naming the first three customers they've personally closed.

### Principle 5 — Spend Time Before Money

> *"Can you do this with time instead of money? Blog posts, social media, personal outreach are free. Only spend money to accelerate what's already working organically."* [pattern-derived]

**Cognitive move:** Cash is reserved for amplifying validated demand, not creating it. Paid acquisition before organic traction is gambling.

**When this fires in discover:** User mentions an ad budget, a paid promo, or "we need to raise to fund growth" without organic proof of pull.

### Principle 6 — Profitability Is The Goal

> *"Does this decision bring you closer to or further from profitability? Are you 'default alive' or 'default dead'? Is this reversible? Avoid irreversible decisions (long leases, big hires, VC terms)."* [pattern-derived]

**Cognitive move:** Reversibility is a feature. Every decision gets a reversibility tag — and irreversible bets need disproportionate scrutiny. *"Hire when it hurts."* [pattern-derived]

**When this fires in discover:** User is making an irreversible move (lease, hire, fundraising round, multi-year commitment) before reversible alternatives are exhausted.

### Principle 7 — Grow At The Speed Of Your Customers

> *"Are your customers asking for this? Or are you guessing? Your company will grow as quickly as your customers determine. The vast majority of small businesses are never eaten by big fish."* [pattern-derived]

**Cognitive move:** Demand pulls, supply doesn't push. If customers aren't asking for the next thing, the next thing is probably the wrong thing.

**When this fires in discover:** Founder is excited about a feature/expansion their existing customers haven't asked for and aren't waiting on.

### Principle 8 — Build The House You Want To Live In

> *"Does this align with your values? Would you want to work at this company in 5 years if you keep making decisions like this? Are you building a business that doesn't own you?"* [pattern-derived]

**Cognitive move:** Long-horizon self-test. *"Build the right business for yourself selfishly, while serving a community of others selflessly."* [pattern-derived]

**When this fires in discover:** User is making compromises they're rationalizing as "just for now" — the kind that compound into a business that owns them.

## 3. The 8-Question Decision Rubric

Eight questions, applied as a structured check on any decision the user brings to discover. Sourced from `minimalist-review`'s decision framework table [pattern-derived]:

| # | Question | What a strong answer looks like |
|---|----------|--------------------------------|
| 1 | Does this serve my community/customers? | Names a specific community + uses recent customer evidence |
| 2 | Is this the simplest approach? | A simpler version was considered and rejected with reason |
| 3 | Does this improve profitability? | Direct path to revenue or margin, not 2 steps removed |
| 4 | Is this reversible if it doesn't work? | Yes, or "no, here's why I'm doing it anyway" with evidence |
| 5 | Am I spending time or money? | Time-first, money only after organic signal |
| 6 | Have customers asked for this? | Quoted requests from named customers |
| 7 | Does this align with my values? | Honest answer; not "yes" by default |
| 8 | Will I still want this in a year? | uses a 12-month frame, not just this quarter |

**Verdict logic** (from `validate-idea` [pattern-derived]): three discover-compatible verdicts:

- **VALIDATED** — Strong signals across the rubric, proceed to MVP. Specifically: principles 4 + 6 + 7 + 8 all green.
- **NEEDS_MORE_VALIDATION** — Mixed signals. Specific next steps to gather evidence (usually: do the manual version for 3-10 people, then re-evaluate).
- **PIVOT** — The idea needs fundamental changes — suggest directions. Trigger: the rubric reveals the user is solving a hypothetical problem, building for a community they don't belong to, or trying to scale before selling.

These verdicts map cleanly onto discover's existing Completion Status Protocol (DONE → VALIDATED, DONE_WITH_CONCERNS → NEEDS_MORE_VALIDATION, BLOCKED/NEEDS_CONTEXT → PIVOT or unblock).

## 4. The Manual-First Process — "Processize Before Productize"

The processize skill [pattern-derived] codifies the manual-first thesis into a six-step process. Discover should treat this as the operator question set for any pre-product idea:

### Step 1 — Strip the Idea to One Sentence

- What is the **one thing** this product does for a customer?
- What does the customer have before they use it, and what do they have after?
- *"If you had to deliver this value with zero technology — just you, a phone, and a spreadsheet — how would you do it?"* [pattern-derived]

### Step 2 — Find Ten Specific People Today

- Who is already trying to solve this problem with a workaround?
- Where do these people hang out? (Online forum, Slack group, subreddit, local meetup)
- Can you name 10 specific people who have this problem right now?

> *"If you can't name 10 people, you don't know your community well enough yet."* [pattern-derived]

### Step 3 — Design the Manual Version

Walk through exactly how you'd deliver the product's value by hand:

- **What does the customer give you?** (An email, a file, a description of what they need)
- **What do you do with it?** (Every step, in order)
- **What do you give back?** (The deliverable)
- **How long does it take you?** (Per customer)

> *"Be specific. 'I process their request' is not a step. 'I open their email, copy the file link, run it through X, format the output, and email it back within 2 hours' is a step."* [pattern-derived]

### Step 4 — Write the Magic Piece of Paper

> *"Write down every step you take on a piece of paper. This is your 'magic piece of paper' — if you went on vacation, someone else could pick it up and keep the business running."* [pattern-derived]

The magic piece of paper includes: trigger · steps (numbered) · tools used at each step · time per customer · handoff back to customer.

### Step 5 — Charge for It

> *"There is a massive difference between free and $1. The zero price effect means free users give you zero signal. Charging even $1 proves someone values what you do."* [pattern-derived]

Reach out to 3 people from the community this week. Deliver the manual version. Collect payment.

### Step 6 — Productize Only When Ready

> *"Only automate what you've proven works manually. Signs you're ready: You've delivered the manual version to 10+ paying customers · You can predict exactly what they'll ask for · The steps on your magic piece of paper haven't changed in weeks · You're spending more time on delivery than on finding customers."* [pattern-derived]

> *"Then — and only then — automate one step at a time. The first thing to automate is whatever takes you the most time per customer."* [pattern-derived]

## 5. The Four Pre-Build Questions

From the book, distilled into `validate-idea` [pattern-derived]. Discover should ask these before any architecture or code discussion:

1. **Can I ship it in the span of a weekend?** First iteration should be prototyped in 2-3 days.
2. **Is it making my customers' life a little better?** That's a minimum viable product.
3. **Is a customer willing to pay me for it?** Profitable from day one.
4. **Can I get feedback quickly?** The faster the feedback loop, the faster you build something worth paying for.

If the answer to any of these is "no," discover should challenge before proceeding.

## 6. Red Flags / Green Flags Rubric

From `validate-idea` [pattern-derived]. Discover scores the user's input against these signals before proceeding to alternatives generation. **If ≥2 red flags OR <2 green flags → push back hard before generating solutions.**

### Red Flags (Do Not Build If…)

> *"- Nobody is currently trying to solve this problem (no existing workarounds)*
> *- You can't name 10 specific people who have this problem*
> *- The only validation is 'my friends think it's a cool idea'*
> *- You need to educate people that they have this problem*
> *- You're building for a community you don't belong to"* [pattern-derived]

Each red flag has a specific diagnostic logic:

| Red flag | What it usually means | Discover follow-up |
|----------|----------------------|--------------------|
| No existing workarounds | Problem isn't painful enough to act on | "If no one's even improvising a solution, why is now the moment?" |
| Can't name 10 specific people | You don't know your community | "Take 30 minutes — name 10. If you can't, that's the work." |
| Only validation is friends | You're in an echo chamber | "Friends are the worst validators. Who's a stranger that paid?" |
| Need to educate the market | Selling a problem, not a solution | "Education-led businesses take 5x longer. Got that runway?" |
| Building for outside community | No native trust or distribution | "Why you, for them? Who in the community is co-founding this?" |

### Green Flags (Worth Pursuing If…)

> *"- People are already paying for inferior solutions*
> *- You've manually solved this for a few people and they loved it*
> *- The community is actively complaining about this problem*
> *- You can describe the customer and their pain point in one sentence*
> *- You're scratching your own itch"* [pattern-derived]

Each green flag is a strong evidence signal — discover should *quote it back* to the user as confirmation when present, not gloss past it.

## 7. Routing Rules

When does each part of this playbook fire? The frame loads on every non-trivial discover invocation, but specific units fire situationally:

| Discover scenario | Lead with |
|-------------------|-----------|
| Pre-product idea, no users yet | The 8-question rubric → red/green flags → manual-first process |
| Plan-review of existing roadmap | The 8 principles applied as audit lenses (especially principles 3, 4, 6) |
| User wants to launch / market / scale | Principle 4 (Sell Before Scale) + Principle 5 (Time Before Money) |
| User considering hire / lease / VC | Principle 6 (Profitability + Reversibility) — push back hard on irreversible moves |
| User excited about new feature | Principle 7 (Speed of Customers) — "did they ask for this?" |
| User burning out / overworked | Principle 8 (House You Want to Live In) — long-horizon self-test |
| Idea-validation cold-start | The four pre-build questions before anything else |
| User ready to ship | Verdict mapping (VALIDATED / NEEDS_MORE / PIVOT) before proceeding |

## 8. Pushback Patterns

These are worked BAD-vs-GOOD applications discover should pattern-match against. Format mirrors office-hours' pushback-patterns format. **Spend disproportionate time here in any given discover session — the patterns are the load-bearing translation from playbook to action.**

### Pattern 1: Premature Productization

- **Trigger:** User opens discover discussing tech stack, framework, database, infrastructure, or features for an unbuilt product.
- **BAD:** "Great question — let's think through whether to use Postgres or MongoDB for your use case."
- **GOOD:** "Stop. Before architecture: have you delivered this value to anyone, by hand, even once? If not, the database choice is procrastination. the operator's rule: processize before productize. What's the manual version look like — you, a phone, an inbox?"
- **Pattern basis:** internal research synthesis.

### Pattern 2: Waitlist-As-Validation

- **Trigger:** User uses waitlist signups, email subscribers, "interest," or LinkedIn engagement as evidence of demand.
- **BAD:** "Nice — 500 signups is a meaningful signal. Let's plan the launch."
- **GOOD:** "500 signups is 500 people who clicked a button. The validation question is binary: has anyone paid? There's a massive difference between free and $1 — the operator specifically calls out waitlist signups as red-flag validation. Pick three of those 500 and try to charge them this week. If three say no, the signal you have is closer to zero than to 500."
- **Pattern basis:** internal research synthesis.

### Pattern 3: Education-Led Business

- **Trigger:** User describes a product whose go-to-market requires teaching the market that the problem exists.
- **BAD:** "Education-led marketing is a long-term play but can build authority — let's plan a content strategy."
- **GOOD:** "If you have to teach people they have this problem, that's a red flag in the minimalist frame — it usually means the problem isn't painful enough for them to act on. The the operator-test: can you name 10 specific people who are *already* trying to solve this problem badly? If you can't, the issue isn't your messaging. It's that the problem isn't urgent. Two paths: find an existing painful adjacent problem you can wedge into, or accept a 5x-longer ramp."
- **Pattern basis:** internal research synthesis.

### Pattern 4: Building For An Outside Community

- **Trigger:** User describes building for a community they don't belong to (e.g., a B2B SaaS founder with no operational background building for ops teams; a non-clinician building for nurses).
- **BAD:** "What user research have you done? We could schedule interviews."
- **GOOD:** "the operator's red flag: building for a community you don't belong to. Interviews are a workaround for absence of native trust and distribution — they help, but they don't fix it. Two operator moves: (1) find a co-founder who IS in the community, (2) become a member of the community for 90 days before building. Which path is feasible? If neither, this is a pivot conversation, not a discover conversation."
- **Pattern basis:** internal research synthesis.

### Pattern 5: Scaling Before Selling

- **Trigger:** User asks discover to help plan paid ads, growth team, automation, or fundraising before they've personally closed customers.
- **BAD:** "Let's think about your CAC targets and acquisition channel mix."
- **GOOD:** "Hold on — sales comes first, marketing second. Manual sales is 99% of early growth in the minimalist frame. How many customers have you personally closed via DMs, emails, or in-person? If the answer is fewer than 10, paid acquisition will burn cash trying to replicate something you haven't proven you can do for free. Time before money. What's the next 5 manual closes look like?"
- **Pattern basis:** internal research synthesis.

## 9. Anti-Patterns (What This Playbook Says NOT To Do)

The frame is opinionated — these are the things minimalist-entrepreneur explicitly rejects. Discover should not *enable* them, even when the user asks for help with them:

1. **Don't productize before processize.** [pattern-derived] If the manual version doesn't exist, software won't fix the underlying gap.

2. **Don't treat "interest" as demand.** "That's interesting," waitlists, and surveys are vanity signals. Only money + behavior count.

3. **Don't build for hypothetical future customers.** *"Are you building for today's customers or hypothetical future ones?"* [pattern-derived]

4. **Don't make irreversible bets early.** Long leases, expensive hires, VC terms with bad clauses are all death-by-irreversibility moves before profitability.

5. **Don't outsource sales.** *"Manual sales = 99% of early growth."* [pattern-derived] Sales is the founder's job until repeatable.

6. **Don't optimize for vanity metrics.** Vanity metrics (signups, MAU without engagement, follower counts) are not the game. Profitability is.

7. **Don't launch.** *"Don't launch. Sell to your first 100 customers first."* [pattern-derived] Big launches are theater; quiet sales are signal.

8. **Don't hire to grow — hire when it hurts.** *"Hire when it hurts."* [pattern-derived] Hiring fixes acute pain, not anticipated growth.

9. **Don't grow faster than customers ask you to.** *"Your company will grow as quickly as your customers determine."* [pattern-derived]

10. **Don't optimize for what 'successful companies' do.** Cargo-culting Stripe, Vercel, or unicorn playbooks is ego-driven, not customer-driven.

## 10. Adaptation Notes

How this playbook adjusts across contexts:

### B2B vs. Consumer

- **B2B:** Manual-first is operationally easier — a B2B "manual version" is often a Google Doc or a Loom video sent to one customer. Charging is straightforward (invoice). Principle 4 (Sell Before Scale) is the highest-leverage rule.
- **Consumer:** Manual-first is harder because consumer apps often need network effects or low-friction self-serve. The translation: Principle 1 (Community First) becomes load-bearing — find the community first, even if "manual delivery" is just a Discord server or a TikTok account before any product exists.

### Bootstrapped vs. Funded

- **Bootstrapped:** The frame is native. Apply directly.
- **Funded (especially VC-backed):** The frame still applies but creates real tension with growth-at-all-costs incentives. Discover should surface the tension explicitly: "this is in conflict with your VC's growth expectations — is that a conversation we need to have with them, or is the playbook wrong for your stage?"

### Indie / Solo vs. Team

- **Indie/Solo:** The default audience for the frame. All eight principles apply with no modification.
- **Team:** Principle 6 (Profitability + Reversibility) gets harder — hiring is a partial-irreversibility move. Principle 8 (House You Want to Live In) gets harder — culture decisions compound. Apply more rigor, not less.

### Pre-Product vs. Post-Product

- **Pre-product:** Lead with the manual-first process and red/green flags rubric. Most weight on principles 1-4.
- **Post-product (existing customers):** Lead with the 8-principle audit. Most weight on principles 6-8.

### Greenfield vs. Pivot

- **Greenfield:** Frame fits cleanly.
- **Pivot:** Frame still applies, but the user has sunk cost they're rationalizing. Principle 7 (Speed of Customers) is the cleanest cut — *what are existing customers asking for?* That's the pivot direction.

### Software-Heavy vs. Service-Heavy

- **Software-heavy products (SaaS, mobile):** "Manual version" is often a stretch — the value IS automation. Translation: the manual version is *the founder doing the automation by hand for 10 customers*. the operator running PayPal payouts manually for the creator-commerce company first creators is the canonical example [pattern-derived].
- **Service-heavy products (consultancy, agency, custom work):** The frame fits natively. The manual version IS the product, and productization is a deliberate later choice — sometimes never.

### Solo Builder vs. Intrapreneur

- **Solo builder:** Direct apply. The community-first / manual-first / sell-before-scale loop is the default.
- **Intrapreneur:** Translate "customer" → internal stakeholder. Translate "sell before scale" → "get a real user / VP commitment before building a v2." Translate "profitability" → "demonstrable ROI before next funding cycle." Translate "magic piece of paper" → "the runbook a teammate could pick up if you went on vacation."

## 11. Open Questions / Known Unknowns

What this playbook *does not* answer well — gaps the verifier flagged:

- **Network-effects products:** The frame is weakest where the value of v1 depends on N users existing simultaneously (marketplaces, social networks). The "deliver value to 10 people manually" test mostly fails here. the operator's frame implicitly accepts this as out of scope ("the vast majority of small businesses are never eaten by big fish") — but discover users may have legitimate marketplace ideas the frame can't directly evaluate.
- **Deep-tech / R&D products:** Multi-year R&D bets (biotech, robotics, novel hardware) cannot be processized. The frame says "don't build these" — but that's not actionable advice when the user is, in fact, building one.
- **Specific dollar thresholds:** The frame is qualitative ("hire when it hurts," "charge $1 to get signal") with no dollar guidance for *how much* to charge or *when* to stop. Practitioner consensus varies; the frame doesn't try to settle it.
- **Two-sided / aggregator businesses:** The minimalist frame is single-customer-focused. Two-sided businesses where you must serve supply and demand simultaneously aren't directly covered.
- **The "sell before scale" rule vs. high-CAC enterprise sales:** Enterprise B2B with 6-12 month sales cycles can't always charge in week 1. The rule still directs the *first close*, but the timeline mismatch means discover should adapt the urgency — the principle holds; the calendar shifts.
- **The frame's edge against Y Combinator's "do things that don't scale" advice:** They overlap heavily but aren't identical. YC permits expensive manual operations as a temporary growth move; minimalist-entrepreneur treats manual as the long-term steady state for many businesses. Worth distinguishing in discover when both come up.
